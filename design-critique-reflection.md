# How to Give and Receive Design Critique

## What is design critique, and why is it a crucial part of the UX process?
Design critique is reviewing a design and giving feedback to improve it. It helps designers spot usability problems, get different perspectives, and make better decisions for users. It helps designers identify usability problems, gain different perspectives, and make better decisions for users.

## What are the key principles of giving helpful, constructive feedback?
Helpful feedback should be clear, respectful, and specific. Instead of saying “this looks bad,” it is better to explain what feels confusing and suggest improvements.

## How should designers receive and apply critique without taking it personally?
Designers should stay open-minded and treat critique as a way to improve the product, not attack the designer. Listening carefully and asking follow-up questions can help turn feedback into useful improvements.

## What are common mistakes when giving or receiving design feedback?
Common mistakes include giving vague comments, focusing only on personal preferences, or becoming defensive during discussions. Ignoring user needs and accessibility is also a major mistake.

# Reflection

## What is the difference between personal opinion and objective UX critique?
Personal opinion is based on individual taste, while UX critique focuses on usability and user needs. For example, saying “I don’t like dark mode” is personal opinion, but saying “the contrast makes buttons hard to see” is objective UX feedback.

## If someone gives vague feedback like "I don’t like this", how would you get more useful input?
I would ask follow-up questions like “What part feels confusing?” or “Was there anything difficult to use?” In Figma, I would also encourage teammates to leave comments directly on the design so the feedback is more specific and easier to understand.

## How can you ensure that design critique sessions are productive rather than overwhelming?
I think critique sessions work best when feedback focuses on the biggest usability issues first instead of criticizing everything at once.
